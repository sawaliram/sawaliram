Global studies on curiosity and questioning

"Global" is an aspirational term, since research on curiosity and questioning largely originates in the Western world. Yet from a broad intellectual perspective curiosity has been studied by such diverse disciplines as philosophy, psychology, cognitive science, neuroscience, child development and education. Research reveals a striking gap between curiosity and questioning.

Research on curiosity focuses on understanding the psychological and neural underpinnings of curiosity, definition and taxonomy, measuring curiosity, and its development in children and adults. This research is undertaken in diverse disciplines such as philosophy, psychology, cognitive science, education, neuroscience etc. The earliest discussion on curiosity were in the domain of Philosophy followed by Psychology. According to Loewenstein (1994), curiosity research in Psychology had undergone two waves of intense activity. The initial phase in the 1960’s attempted to understand the psychological underpinnings of curiosity. The second phase in the 1970’s and 80’s attempted to measure curiosity and asses its dimensionality. Curiosity studies has shown a recent resurgence in Neuroscience with the advent of Functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging techniques, where the neural, biological and cognitive mechanism underlying curiosity has been studied. Experimental studies in neuroscience has shown that midbrain is highly active in curious individuals. Studies in education explore the interaction between curiosity and learning. Curiosity is considered as a form of intrinsic motivation in fostering exploration and learning. Researchers have also examined the relation between curiosity and creativity, found to be positively correlated.

Education researchers who observe classroom interactions continually remark on the lack of student questioning. The dearth of research on student questioning is attributed to the fact that ‘investigators can scarcely find any student questions’.2 We do however know that students ask questions, covertly or overtly, when facilitative conditions are provided to them.4
